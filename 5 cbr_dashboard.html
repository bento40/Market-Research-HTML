Booking.com dominance: One channel delivers 61% of net revenue and 56% of room nights. This creates massive dependency risk. If Booking.com changed commission terms or ranking algorithms, the impact would be catastrophic. Diversification is urgent.
Direct booking paradox: Direct has the lowest ARR (฿3,123) but highest net margin since there's zero commission. At current OTA ARRs, a direct booking at ฿3,400 would net more than a Booking.com booking at ฿4,018 after 15% commission (฿3,415 net). Price parity with a small direct-booking bonus could shift meaningful volume.
Commission savings opportunity: Shifting just 15% of OTA volume to direct could save approximately ฿2.2M per year in commission costs — equivalent to hiring 5+ additional staff or funding a complete website redesign and marketing campaign.

Methodology
Income is estimated by multiplying four signals: (1) Country median household income (PPP-adjusted) as the base. (2) Travel distance multiplier — long-haul leisure travelers to Thailand self-select into the top 20–30% of their country's income distribution (×2.2 for Europe/Americas, ×1.8 for East Asia/Middle East, ×1.5 for ASEAN). (3) Age-income curve — peak earning years 45–54 get ×1.3, younger brackets get lower multipliers. (4) Room rate premium — guests paying above-average rates (>฿4,000/night) get an additional ×1.2 uplift. These are estimates, not actuals — useful for segmentation and positioning, not financial planning.

Two-tier guest base: CBR serves two distinct income segments. The dominant group (46%) is $150K+ Western European, American, and Oceanian households — upper-middle to affluent. The secondary group (~27%) is $30–75K from China, Russia, India, and Thailand — value-conscious travelers. Pricing, amenities, and marketing should acknowledge both segments rather than targeting a single tier.
Premium positioning is supported: Nearly half your guests come from households earning $150K+. These guests are accustomed to quality and will pay for it — they're choosing CBR at ฿3,700–4,600/night, but comparable wellness-positioned resorts charge ฿6,000–10,000. The rebrand to wellness-forward positioning is well-aligned with this income demographic.
Rate gap vs. income capacity: Swiss guests (est. $207K household income) pay an ARR of only ฿3,846 — well below what their income would support. Danish guests ($219K income) pay ฿4,566. The spread suggests significant headroom to increase rates for high-income nationalities, especially with improved product and positioning.
